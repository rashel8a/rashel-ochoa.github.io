Ultra-personalized travel

Roamr makes it easy to find curated experiences and like-minded travelers to prepare travelers for their global adventures ahead.

Summary

Why Roamr?

Traveling shouldn’t impede the ability for a person to be the fullest expression of themselves. Whether it's finding experiences that suit your personality or meeting the type of people that broaden your perspectives, travel should serve as a gateway to charting your own personal journey – whatever that may be.

With the number of solo travelers skyrocketing over the last couple of years, many eager wanderers are asking themselves how they can find better, less frequented terrains and more diverse cultural experiences. Roamr is a mobile application that changes the way people find and experience travel by customizing the user’s travel experiences and friendly encounters to them completely.

CHAPTER 1 | OVERVIEW AT A GLANCE

The challenge

Defining the problems

Travelers of all types — solo travelers, backpackers, couple travelers, etc. — headed to a new destination are frequently faced with the following two questions:

  • Will they meet interesting people to adventure and travel with?
  • Which experiences and activities are worth their fragile time?


While travelers can easily find people to adventure with through hostels, booking tours, etc. they are often not the best match in terms of the ideal traveler for their personality or travel type. Rather than joining tourist activities created for generic tourists and meeting random people along the way, my research shows that travelers want to embark on their own off the beaten path experiences and meet like-minded travelers that directly relate to their personal interests and travel styles. With a growing number of travelers using dating apps to meet friends abroad, users have clearly shown that there's a market need for a more targeted and curated travel experience.

The solution

Smart travel suggestions for an ultra-personalized user experience

Today, there is no question that travel must be modernized for a mobile experience and a social world. Roamr addresses this problem by using machine learning to find curated experiences and connect like-minded travelers that suit the user’s personality, motivations, and adventure type with just a few simple swipes. Roamr completely customizes travel experiences to the user and helps them find travelers based off their social travel preferences so they can focus on what’s most important — making travel memories that last a lifetime.

CHAPTER 2 | DISCOVERY PHASE

User survey

Getting to know the users

To better understand the people who’d be using this product, I conducted a user survey to identify what motivated and frustrated my users with regards to finding enjoyable, tailored travel experiences and making plans with people they met along the way. In order to get a good grasp of my target audience, I needed to learn the following:

  • How passionate were they about traveling on long or short trips;
  • Had they gone on a solo travel and/or backpacking trip before;
  • What were their main concerns while traveling;
  • Did they typically travel with friends, family or by themselves;
  • What features within a travel app would they find most valuable;
  • What would they like to know about travelers before meeting and adventuring with them;
  • How often do they travel and for how long;
  • How did they typically find experiences and places to visit;
  • How do they meet other travelers while exploring new places;
  • What apps (if any) did they use to meet other travelers;


The survey questions were completed by 38 respondents.

User survey

Testing my assumptions

Before analyzing the survey results, I anticipated that this project would focus on the “social” aspect of a travel mobile app. I thought users would be interested in matching with like-minded travelers “Tinder-style” either for a romantic affiliation or friendship. Travelers could then follow each other to view their social travel posts, maintain friendships, and get notified if they were in the same city. The app would target female solo travelers since my initial online research and personal travel experiences showed that women’s primary concerns while traveling were safety. To see if my assumptions were correct, I sent out a selective user survey to frequent travelers. Needless to say, the results surprised me.

User Research Highlights

Travel concerns & behaviors

75% of respondents’ key concerns when traveling alone are safety & smart planning.
Respondents listed Google, TripAdvisor, Yelp, and Pinterest as the top trip planning resources with 95% making at least a basic itinerary before traveling.
90% wanted to meet travelers strictly for friendships, 65% preferred meeting with 2-3 travelers at once, and 50% chose to meet both single & couple travelers.

My user research showed that respondents were underwhelmed about matching with travelers by swiping left or right simply because that process felt impersonal, overdone, and too focused on the dating scene — which was not what travelers were looking for. They were more interested in inviting people to join their adventurous plans because it provided insights into the user’s personality and generated a natural opportunity for the traveler to reach out to the user if they had common interests.

User interviews & survey results

What the users actually want

To confirm the results of the user survey, I interviewed two backpackers who’d taken several round-the-world trips. Below is a list of their combined opinions and motivations:

  • Users wanted to meet with more than one person when exploring new places
  • Users preferred meeting travelers by inviting them to join their plans rather than matching "Tinder-style"
  • Single travelers were open to exploring new places with couple travelers even though many couples felt ostracized from the “traveler scene”


An important user research discovery showed that many travelers were tired of joining pre-established travel tours created by hostels or booking tours because they did not get a unique travel experience catered to their motives. It was easy to join these tours bandwagon-style because it required no planning and led to meeting new people, but they felt that they were too generic and apathetic.

Problem insights

Pinpointing user frustrations

To recap, here were the most prevalent frustrations that continued to resurface when it came to exploring new places:

  • Travelers are used to seeking out travel activities that fit their needs rather than having them pushed to them based on their travel interests
  • They don’t want to use dating apps to meet people abroad because they feel superficial and forced
  • They are looking for tailored experiences that they can easily plan on their own rather than booking “pre-packaged trips” organized by hostels/hotels/tours
  • They felt that it was difficult to meet people who aligned with their travel style because most travel friendships are made purely by chance

It was clear that my pre-made user assumptions were wrong. Instead of focusing on creating an app that matched like-minded travelers "Tinder-style", I decided to deviate from this idea to focus on allowing the user to receive personalized adventures based on their interests and then invite nearby travelers to explore these new and diverse experiences together.

Target audience

Who are the users?

While the majority of survey respondents were women between the ages of 21 and 28, the survey results showed that it wasn’t just women who were interested in using a travel app to find new experiences and meet diverse people while wandering to new places. It was single travelers, couple travelers, men, and non-binary genders who all found value in this app. This leading theme of designing inclusive products is what made me switch gears to focus on designing a travel app for all types of people within the ages of 21 and 34, regardless of their travel personality type or preferences.

Competitive analysis

Where does Roamr sit in the marketplace?

Travello, Couchsurfing, and TripAdvisor – three major competitors in the travel industry – were the competitors I chose to analyze. With customization to the user being key, I had to ask myself – what were competitors doing to customize their experience to the user?

While Travello does request the user’s travel interests at the beginning of its onboarding process, it doesn’t continue learning about the user’s preferences as the user interacts with the app’s features. Similarly, Couchsurfing’s “Hangouts” feature doesn’t allow its users to filter the types of hangouts they’d like to join. Instead, users must scroll through the list of hangout requests to find an activity they want to join.

I also found that it was difficult to identify the top-rated experiences within both Travello and Trip Advisor, especially when it came to finding experiences off the beaten path. What if users could search for top-voted experiences in a variety of ways using just a couple simple swipes? What if they tried (and loved) a new adventure sport experience and wanted to visually search for similar activities to it in different parts of the world? What if they could see what a place will look like before actually going to it? By incorporating new forms of search and customization features, the user could broaden their scope and target of experiences they’d like to explore.

User personas

Bringing the users to life

After analyzing the results from the user survey, I’d created three types of users that would embody the full range of Roamr's audience.

To represent each of these user types, I created Rory, Raya and Liz, and Joaquin. By giving real-life motivations, frustrations, faces, and names to these personas, I could later ask myself — what would Raya and Liz think of this design decision? Would Joaquin and Rory find this addition useful or trivial? Would this addition serve their needs? I knew that asking these questions throughout the design process was key to reaching the MVP.

Rory

Solo Offbeat Adventurer, 26

"

Solo travel is a fast track to personal growth, but you have to be smart about it."

Motivations –

As a grade school teacher, Rory uses her summer time off as a chance to disconnect and take long trips abroad. Rory enjoys traveling alone for self reflection, but she also likes to meet up with female solo travelers to travel safely in groups and to make new friends. She relies on small offbeat travel blogs to get advice for her next adventure and avoids tourist traps.

Frustrations –

Because she’s determined to find places off the beaten path, Rory spends a large portion of her precious travel time researching online. She’d like to be able to find places that match her budget, mood, aesthetic environment style, and physical activities but has trouble finding apps or websites that can help her find places according to her needs.

Joaquin

Solo Budget Traveler, 28

"

For me, travel is more about the stories you hear than the places you see."

Motivations –

While Joaquin has been crippled with debt for years, his love for meeting people and capturing photographs of the world far exceed his concern for his bank account. He frequently travels alone, meeting people in hostels along the way to join budget-friendly group activities. As a photographer, one of Joaquin’s favorite activities is finding interesting people, taking their photos, and hearing their stories.

Frustrations –

While he wants to spend more time exploring tourist spots with travelers, he isn’t a planner and tends to follow the group. He frequently befriends hostel goers who like to party and ends up missing opportunities to see places he’d like to go to frequently.

Raya & Liz

Foodie Couple, 27 & 29

"

For us, travel is a gentle reminder to always love and be yourself."

Motivations –

As digital nomads, Raya & Liz frequently take short trips to Southeast Asian countries relishing any opportunity to try spicy, flavorful foods from different countries and cultures. They prefer getting food recommendations from friends and locals to avoid the tourists. And while they love each other’s presence, they like to switch it up by seeking out single and couple friends of all types to spice up the conversation over delicious ethnic foods.

Frustrations –

As a lesbian digital nomad couple, Raya and Liz find it difficult to meet travelers who are a part of the LGBTQ digital nomad community. Because of this, they frequently use BumbleBFF to find people to hang out with and use foodie blogs to explore new places.

A couple insights unearthed from testing the wireframes showed that the current onboarding process was too long, there was an unfamiliarity with the activity feed buttons displayed immediately upon entering the dashboard, and the purpose of the Earth spectacles feature was unclear upon accessing the feature, prompting users to undergo excessive clicking and confusion.

Iterations based on testing

Refining for a shallow learning curve

After observing users struggle through some major areas within my prototype, I realized that the following educational components were necessary to significantly reduce the margin for error.

1

Reducing churn by streamlining the onboarding

Ok so, I’ll admit I wanted to substitute a chatbot for the traditional form-filling onboarding process because it’s conversational and fun. But after getting feedback from users, I realized that I’d made a mistake by placing my motivations before theirs. And in doing so I learned a valuable lesson — always put the users first.

Yes, I’d designed an entire 20 screens specifically for this exciting onboarding process. But did it take too long for them to complete? Yes. Was the delight of having a conversational chatbot more important the users time? No. So I switched it back to the form-filling onboarding process which saved approximately two minutes of the user’s time.

The evolution of the interests personalization picker

For the app to suggest the user the right types of experiences and people to meet, it needs to understand what the user loves to do while traveling. Instead of selecting from an established, rigid list of interests, I wanted the app to learn and respond to the user’s selected choices. So if they chose “adventure sports” as a broad activity that they enjoyed, they could then find narrower topics underneath it like “paragliding” or “sky diving." Adding tooltips to reduce cognitive overload

Several users had overlooked the placement and function of the reviews posted by travelers immediately after onboarding. Later on some stated that they’d felt “thrown in” to the dashboard immediately after being onboarded. To reduce confusion and cognitive overload upon entering the dashboard, I added a friendly “welcome” screen stating what the user was looking at followed by a series of quick tool tips showcasing the voting and save to bucketlist features.

4

Educating users on the earth spectacles feature

My hunch that the earth spectacles feature would come across as obscure was indeed, correct. Having garnered high attention within the survey results, this feature allows users to see the appearance of an experience and its rating before going to it. The problem was, users were having a tough time understanding how to use it. But after learning about the feature’s utility, users found value in being able to swipe around the screen to view different angles of the experience's location and read the top voted reviews. So to clear confusion on its purpose, I included nudge-focused tooltips showing users how they can utilize this feature to their advantage via swipe gestures.

5

Optimizing the date selection process

In ideating methods to streamline the date selection process, I decided to completely eliminate the date picker scroller and calendar within the “Make a Plan” flow. Because most users wanted to make a plan to meet with traveler(s) within the next few days, I chose to display “quick day” tags that showed days like “tomorrow” or “Tuesday” rather than specific dates. If the user wanted to make a plan a month ahead of time or within a particular date range, they could do so by selecting the “specific date” or “date range” sections.

CHAPTER 4 | BRANDING & IDENTITY

Conceptual ideation

Creating a brand with an inclusive theme

Roamr is a social travel platform enabling travelers of all types to find experiences and people that align with their interests while on the road. Roamr empowers travelers to be themselves no matter where they are. It’s a friendly travel enabler connecting people from all over the world to find personalized experiences and memories that last a lifetime.

To Roam implies a wandering or traveling over a large area, especially as prompted by restlessness or a driving curiosity to understand…”

Logo design

A logo that spurs a desire for movement

After conducting word association exercises to collect possible brand names, I ended up with three options: Roamr, Veer, and Wayfinder. In the end, I chose Roamr because, unlike the other two, it doesn't focus on the app. Rather, it describes the user's frame of mind – a traveler's mantra if you will – for those who are in constant need of movement.

Roamr’s primary logo charts the rewarding journey a traveler undergoes, both in distance and in personal growth, when going from their current location to end destination. The circle represents the traveler’s current location and the pin represents their destination with a dotted line or journey connecting the two.

Roamr’s mark highlights the last letter of the logo name which is the same as its primary letter. The last letter was chosen as its mark to highlight the importance of the traveler’s end destination and the journey that transforms the user along the way.

Color palette

Getting users excited about their journey ahead

Because the audience is between the ages of 21 and 34, I chose to use contrasted blue tones and pastel blue tints harmoniously to transmit soothing and energetic sensations reminiscent of the sea and sky – two of the primary forms of transportation to transoceanic places. It also conveys a trustworthy brand image especially for travelers going to foreign places and adventuring with friends they haven’t met yet.

Typography

A clean typeface to go with the brand

Apercu and Glacial Indifference were selected as the primary typefaces for Roamr because their simplicities and subtle differences harmonize well together. Apercu’s grotesque sans-serif typeface is full of character, displaying subtle quirks that complement the simplicity of Glacial Indifference’s orderly geometric sans-serif typeface.

Conclusion

Looking back & beyond

What I learned –

Stay focused on the MVP

Roamr was a passion project I’d been planning on creating ever since I’d returned from my backpacking journey around the world. Even with so many apps at my fingertips, I still felt that many apps didn’t meet my demands when attempting to find my kind of experiences, places, and like-minded people while on the road. I was so passionate about this project however, I ended up inputting more features than I believe was truly needed for the MVP. While the purpose of Roamr is to be robust and offer features for different user types, being able to draw a line between the "must-haves" or highest priority features and "nice-haves" or medium priority features is critical. Nevertheless, Roamr’s purpose is intentional and robust in its motive of providing people with the experiences they deserve.

Let the users dictate the project's direction

I also learned the importance of letting users help you guide the direction of an application. While I had initially thought that Roamr would serve as a platform to match with other travelers and stay connected with them, I’d found that users wanted more than that. They wanted to ease the research portion of their travel process by having experiences suggested to them. While I had a targeted intention for the primary audience, app features, and overall branding at the beginning of this project, I completed it with the project headed in a completely different direction. And that’s more than ok! It means there’s a market need. Because this was a passion project, all my excitement and energy was invested in each phase of the design process and features. So much so, that I let myself throw out the basic UX design principles by holding onto some design decisions and user stories that I was most passionate about — like the filtering transition process and the nearby friend locator which would notify you if your traveler friends were in the same city as you. I learned that I need to keep myself in check, to identify whether my own biases and opinions about what users want are taking over. I found that if I’m unsure of this question, testing the application is one of the best ways to find my answer.

Additions beyond the MVP

If I could expand beyond the scope of my MVP (and if I had more time), I’d design the user flows that are displayed within many screens on the prototype but were not clickable. Some of these included the ability to “explore travelers” on the “explore” tab, post to a travel forum rather than just an experience review, upload an image to view similar experiences to it using the visual search, and search for specific experiences in specific locations for those who’d like to plan ahead of their trips.
